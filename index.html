Legal Infrastructure
Priority 1 of 5 — Complete by Day 14
🔴 Critical — Do First
Engage a Healthcare Attorney
This is the single highest-leverage action in the entire 90-day plan. Everything else — engagement agreements, advocate contracts, liability scope — flows from this. Do not draft your own agreements. Healthcare advocacy has specific liability nuances in Florida that a general attorney will miss.
  • Search for Florida attorneys specializing in healthcare law or private nursing practice — not general business law
  • Ask Pat's professional network (FAPHN, FPHA, FGCU advisory board) for referrals to attorneys who have worked with private nurse practitioners or concierge health practices
  • Schedule consultations with 2–3 candidates; ask specifically about experience with 1099 nurse contractor models and health advocacy scope-of-practice in Florida
  • Budget $3,000–$6,000 for initial document drafting; this is not the place to cut costs
Person Required Pat or family leadership selects and engages the attorney — this is a relationship and judgment call
🔴 Critical — Do First
Draft the Client Engagement Agreement
This document defines the commercial relationship, the scope of service, liability boundaries, and termination terms. It protects NHC from scope creep and protects the client from unmet expectations. The current client should be retroactively covered by a signed version of this document.
  • Work with attorney to define the four critical elements: scope of service (what advocates do and do not do), compensation and payment terms, termination clause (30 days written notice recommended), and liability limitation
  • Include a one-page plain-English "What We Do / What We Don't Do" summary as an attachment — this prevents 80% of future disputes
  • Define what happens when a client has a hospitalization requiring more than 20 hours in a month — establish an overage rate in the agreement ($200/hr recommended)
  • Have the existing client sign the finalized version; frame it as formalizing the relationship professionally

Insurance Coverage
Priority 2 of 5 — Complete by Day 14
🔴 Critical
Secure Professional Liability Insurance (E&O)
Professional liability — also called Errors and Omissions or malpractice insurance — covers NHC if a client claims the service caused harm or failed to deliver. This is non-negotiable before a second client is signed. The existing client creates retroactive liability risk until this is in place.
  • Contact an insurance broker with healthcare specialty — search specifically for brokers who work with private nursing practices, patient advocates, or case management businesses in Florida
  • Request quotes for both a company policy (covers NHC LLC) and individual advocate policies (required in advocate contracts)
  • Ask about professional liability for health advocacy specifically — some policies exclude "navigation" services; ensure your policy covers the exact activities NHC performs
  • Estimated cost: $2,500–$5,000/year for company policy at this scale; have the attorney review the policy before binding
  • Reference: National Association of Healthcare Advocacy (NAHAC) often has insurance resources and preferred brokers for member advocates
👤 Person Required Pat selects broker, binds policy, reviews with attorney
🔵 High
General Liability + Business Owner's Policy
Covers bodily injury and property damage during home visits and in-person service delivery. If an advocate is at a client's home and something goes wrong, this protects the company. Bundle with professional liability for efficiency.
  • Request a Business Owner's Policy (BOP) that combines general liability with property coverage — most small healthcare practices use this structure
  • Confirm that home visit activities are covered (some policies exclude residential settings)
  • Estimated cost: $800–$1,500/year bundled with professional liability

Advocate Recruitment & Onboarding
Priority 1 of 4 — The Revenue Multiplier
🔴 Critical
Interview and Select Advocate #2
Approach this hire with the same rigor you would use to choose a business partner — because in a trust-based service like this, a bad advocate can permanently damage the brand. Pat's intuition about character is the most important filter. Credentials matter; integrity matters more.
  • Conduct structured interviews with the 3 candidates identified in Days 1–30; use a consistent set of scenario questions, not just a resume review
  • Key interview questions: "Describe a time when a patient's family member was unreasonable — how did you handle it?" / "How would you tell a family something they didn't want to hear?" / "What would you do if a client asked you to stay an extra hour beyond your agreed scope?"
  • Conduct a 2-hour shadow day: have each finalist spend time with Pat and the existing client (with client's consent) to assess fit, communication style, and instinct
  • Run a background check on the selected candidate — minimum: criminal, sex offender registry, FL nursing license verification (if applicable), professional reference checks (2 minimum)
  • Trust Pat's gut above all qualifications — the people doing this work will be alone in seniors' homes. Character is the credential that matters most.

Pat's Role Transition

🔴 Critical
Transition Client #1 to Advocate #2 or #3

  • Overlap period: both Pat and the new advocate attend 2 home visits together before Pat steps back
  • Pat remains available to the client family via phone for the first 30 days post-transition — make this explicit in the family communication

  • Section 1 — NHC Mission & Values: Why we exist, what we believe, what we will never compromise. The three non-negotiables: client interests first, no gifts ever, scope of practice is absolute
  • Section 2 — The NHC Service Standard: Complete description of every service component — home visit protocol, medication chart process, appointment attendance guidelines, family update format, provider communication standards
  • Section 3 — Client Onboarding: Step-by-step onboarding checklist. Week 1 and Week 2 protocols. The HIPAA authorization process. How to conduct the initial home safety assessment
  • Section 4 — Communication Standards: Weekly update template with examples. How to communicate bad news to a family. How to handle a family member who disagrees with the provider's recommendation. Escalation protocol — what to do and who to call when a situation exceeds the advocate's scope
  • Section 5 — Scope of Practice: What advocates do and do not do. Specific scenarios with correct responses. What constitutes immediate grounds for dismissal
  • Section 6 — Documentation & Reporting: Internal reporting template. How to maintain the client service log. Confidentiality and records retention requirements
  • Section 7 — NHC Administrative Procedures: Invoicing process (advocate's monthly invoice to NHC), check-in schedule with NHC leadership, performance review process

Conduct First Formal Quality Reviews
By Day 90, Advocate #2 has been actively serving clients for 4–6 weeks. A formal quality review — structured, documented, and constructive — establishes the performance management culture from the start. Everyone benefits from explicit feedback; don't assume good work is good enough without checking.
  • Review all weekly reports filed by Advocate #2 — look for completeness, communication quality, and any patterns in what's being noticed or missed
  • Call the client family served by Advocate #2 — a 10-minute check-in with a simple question: "Is there anything we could be doing better for your family?" This is the most valuable quality data NHC has
  • Conduct a 45-minute structured review session with Advocate #2: what's working well, what has been challenging, any scope or boundary situations that arose, Pat's specific observations and suggestions
  • Document the review and have the advocate sign off — this creates a professional development record and demonstrates NHC's commitment to quality

Formalize the Shell Point Referral Relationship
By Day 90, NHC should have one or more clients within Shell Point, Pat has had multiple meetings with community staff, and the trust foundation exists to have a more formal conversation about NHC's role as a preferred resource for the community.
  • Request a formal meeting with Shell Point's Director of Resident Services or equivalent — come prepared with a one-page overview of NHC, client outcomes (even 1–2 client stories without names), and a proposed referral framework
  • Proposed ask: permission to be listed in Shell Point's community resource guide as a recommended health advocacy service. This is the minimum; do not push for an exclusive arrangement at this stage
  • Offer to do a 30-minute educational presentation for Shell Point residents and their families: "What is a Health Advocate and Do You Need One?" — this builds awareness and trust simultaneously
  • Follow up with written thank-you to every Shell Point contact who has supported NHC — handwritten note from Pat, brief and genuine

🔵 High
Build the Professional Referral Network
Beyond Shell Point, there are five categories of professionals in Southwest Florida who regularly encounter families in exactly the situation NHC serves. Building relationships with even 5–10 of these professionals creates a referral engine that generates clients without advertising.
  • Category 1 — Elder Law Attorneys: Search the Lee County Bar Association for attorneys specializing in elder law and estate planning. Schedule introductory lunches; bring the client marketing deck. These attorneys hear "my parent's health is getting complicated" every week.
  • Category 2 — Geriatric Financial Advisors: Many fee-only financial advisors in Fort Myers and Naples specialize in retirement planning for seniors. They advise on long-term care costs — NHC's service is directly relevant to their clients' planning.
  • Category 3 — Primary Care Physicians: The PCP of a Shell Point resident with a complex care situation is often frustrated by the lack of coordination. A nurse advocate who attends appointments and follows up is a physician's ally. Target 3–5 PCPs who see large numbers of Shell Point patients.
  • Category 4 — Hospital Social Workers: Beyond discharge planners, social workers in the oncology, cardiology, and geriatric departments at Lee Memorial are consistently working with families facing the exact situation NHC addresses.
  • Category 5 — Community Health Fairs and Senior Centers: Lee County Senior Services, the Area Agency on Aging for Southwest Florida, and local senior centers host events where NHC's target demographic gathers. Request a table at 2–3 events in the next 60 days.

Create a Client Testimonial Process
The most powerful marketing NHC has — more than any deck or website — is a real family saying "this changed everything for us." Start collecting these stories now, with the proper consent framework, so they are available for marketing by Month 4.
  • After 60+ days with an active client, schedule a 15-minute check-in call with the adult child — not to discuss the client's care, but to ask: "What has been the most valuable thing NHC has done for your family?"
  • If the response is positive (it should be), ask permission to share their story (without names, or with names if they're willing) in NHC marketing materials
  • Draft a 2–3 sentence testimonial based on their words; send it to them for approval before using it anywhere
  • Even one authentic testimonial on the NHC website converts more prospects than a dozen feature bullet points

Begin Content Strategy — LinkedIn & Email
Pat's expertise, story, and passion for elder advocacy is content that writes itself. Two LinkedIn posts per month and one email newsletter per month — thoughtful, personal, and educational — build NHC's reputation and organic reach more effectively than any paid advertising at this stage.
  • Content themes: "What I learned from 45 years of nursing that no one tells families"; "The question you should ask at every doctor's appointment"; "What a hospital discharge planner can't do — and who can"; "What I wish every family of an elderly parent knew"
  • Write in Pat's authentic voice — not corporate, not clinical. Personal, direct, and warm. Pat's voice IS the brand at this stage.
  • Two LinkedIn posts per month (one personal story, one practical tip for families); one email newsletter per quarter to the NHC list (start building the email list via the website contact form from Day 1)
  • Do not outsource this to a generic copywriter. Have a family member assist Pat in turning her spoken thoughts into written content — interview Pat on the phone, transcribe, polish. This preserves authenticity.

90-Day Financial & Strategy Review
Priority 4 of 4 — Measure, Adjust, Plan Forward
🔵 High
90-Day Business Review
At Day 90, NHC has real data — real clients, real costs, real referral sources, real conversion experiences. This review is about using that data to make better decisions for the next 90 days than were made in the first 90.
  • Revenue: What is actual monthly recurring revenue at Day 90 vs. the plan? What is the trend line?
  • Clients: What was the source of each signed client? What is the average time from first inquiry to signed agreement? What inquiries did not convert and why?
  • Advocates: Is $50/hr competitive? Is the 1099 model working as expected? What is the advocate's experience of the role — are there friction points to address?
  • Pricing: Has any prospect objected to the $4,000 price point? Should a tiered service option be explored (e.g., $2,500/month for 10 hours) to expand the addressable market?
  • Next 90 days: Based on the data, set specific targets for Days 91–180: number of clients, number of advocates, referral relationships, and revenue

Plan the Expansion to Second Community
Shell Point is the beachhead. By Day 90, if NHC has 2–4 clients there, the learnings are transferable to the next community. Moorings Park in Naples or a large Fort Myers community (Cypress Cove, The Arlington) are logical next targets.

  • Plan the community entry strategy: same playbook as Shell Point — personal relationships first, marketing deck leave-behind, offer to present to residents, no hard sell
  • Determine: does NHC need a third advocate before expanding to a second community, or can existing capacity serve both?
